# The Kite Runner — Book Review

## Summary

*The Kite Runner* by Khaled Hosseini follows Amir, a young boy growing up in Kabul, and his deep yet unequal friendship with Hassan, the son of his father’s servant. Their early years are filled with small joys — storytelling, companionship, and the thrill of kite running, which becomes a powerful symbol throughout the book.

Amir constantly seeks his father’s approval, while Hassan, loyal and kind, stands by him without question. However, a single moment of fear and betrayal during their childhood changes everything. Amir makes a choice he cannot undo, and instead of confronting it, he distances himself from Hassan, setting off a chain of consequences that shape both their lives.

As political unrest grows and Afghanistan changes under the weight of war and conflict, Amir and his father are forced to flee to the United States. There, Amir grows up, builds a new life, and tries to move forward. Yet, despite the distance and time, his past continues to haunt him quietly.

Years later, a call from an old family friend brings Amir back into the shadows of his childhood. He is offered a chance to return to a war-torn Afghanistan, uncover truths he never knew, and face the weight of his guilt. What follows is not just a physical journey, but an emotional one — about confronting the past, seeking redemption, and understanding what it truly means to be “good again.”

## My Thoughts

This is one of those books that doesn’t try too hard to impress, yet leaves a deep impact.

What worked for me was how human the story felt. Amir is not a perfect character, and that’s what makes him believable. His fear, his choices, and the way he carries guilt felt very real. At times, I found myself frustrated with him, but I also understood where he was coming from.

Hassan, on the other hand, brings a quiet strength to the story. His loyalty and innocence contrast sharply with Amir’s inner conflict, making their relationship even more powerful.

The writing is simple, but emotionally heavy. It doesn’t rely on dramatic language, yet it delivers moments that hit hard and stay with you.

It took me a few days to finish this book, not because it was slow, but because I often had to pause and sit with what I had just read. Some chapters are emotionally intense, and rushing through them didn’t feel right. I found myself reading in short stretches, reflecting in between, which made the experience more immersive.

## What Stayed With Me

More than the events, it’s the emotional weight of the story that stayed.

The idea that a single moment can shape an entire life.  
The way guilt quietly grows over time.  
And the possibility — however difficult — of redemption.

Also, the portrayal of Afghanistan stood out. It felt like seeing a place through someone’s memories rather than through news headlines. That contrast made the story even more impactful.

## Final Review

This is not an easy or light read, but it’s definitely a meaningful one.

It makes you reflect, question, and feel uncomfortable in a way that good books often do.

**Rating: 8/10**

- **Page Turner:** No (but deeply engaging)
- **Genre:** Historical Fiction / Literary Fiction / Drama
- **Recommended For:** Readers who enjoy emotional, character-driven stories
- **Re-read Value:** Medium
- **Pace:** Moderate
